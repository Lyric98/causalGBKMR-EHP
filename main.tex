\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Background.} Longitudinal environmental health studies measure multiple correlated chemicals at multiple visits, yet existing mixture analysis software operates in a cross-sectional framework and cannot accommodate time-varying confounding. The g-BKMR method combines the g-formula with Bayesian kernel machine regression to address this gap, but no production-quality R package has been available.

\noindent\textbf{Objectives.} We present \pkg{causalGBKMR}, an R package implementing the g-BKMR estimator for joint and single-component causal effects of time-varying environmental mixtures, together with an optional fast-BKMR engine for large samples. We aim to (i) validate the implementation, (ii) demonstrate its application in a real cohort, and (iii) characterise its computational scalability.

\noindent\textbf{Methods.} \pkg{causalGBKMR} integrates Bayesian kernel machine regression with the g-formula and supports component-wise variable selection, posterior summarisation of dose-response functions, and a variational fast-BKMR back-end. We evaluate the package along three use cases: (1) replication of a previously published simulation study; (2) application to the Strong Heart Study (SHS), a prospective cohort of American Indian adults, to quantify the joint effect of six urinary metals at three visits on fasting blood glucose; and (3) a computational benchmark contrasting standard and fast-BKMR engines at sample sizes representative of large environmental cohorts.

\noindent\textbf{Results.} The package reproduced the published g-BKMR simulation results within Monte Carlo error, confirming implementation validity. In the SHS application ($n = 679$, six metals, three visits), the joint mixture effect on log fasting glucose was positive and significant at the 75th vs.\ 25th percentile contrast (ACE $= 1.01$ SD; 95\% CrI: $0.72$, $1.33$), with zinc identified as the dominant contributor. The fast-BKMR engine processed $n = 6{,}000$ observations $15\times$ faster than standard BKMR at the same sample size ($49$ minutes vs.\ $37$ hours for the full g-formula pipeline), with nearly identical posterior estimates.

\noindent\textbf{Discussion.} \pkg{causalGBKMR} provides a unified, validated, and scalable implementation of causal inference for time-varying environmental mixtures, lowering the barrier to applied causal mixture analysis in environmental epidemiology.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Bayesian kernel machine regression; causal inference; environmental mixtures; g-formula; longitudinal data; R package; Strong Heart Study.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Longitudinal environmental health studies now routinely measure multiple correlated chemicals at multiple visits, creating demand for methods that can estimate the joint causal effect of time-varying exposure mixtures on health outcomes\citep{stafoggia_review_2017,taylor_prime_2022}.
Several mixture methods---including weighted quantile sum (WQS) regression\citep{carrico_wqs_2015}, quantile g-computation\citep{keil_qgcomp_2020}, Bayesian WQS\citep{colicino_bwqs_2020}, and Bayesian kernel machine regression (BKMR)\citep{bobb_bkmr_2015,bobb_bkmr_2018}---have become widely adopted, each with well-maintained R software.
However, these tools are designed for cross-sectional settings and do not address time-varying confounding, the setting in which intermediate covariates (e.g., body mass index) are affected by past exposures and simultaneously confound future exposure--outcome relationships.

\citet{chai_gbkmr} addressed this gap by proposing g-BKMR, which embeds BKMR within the g-formula framework\citep{robins_gformula_1986} to yield valid causal estimates under time-varying confounding.
The key idea is to decompose the causal estimand---the average causal effect (ACE) of shifting the entire exposure trajectory from one level $\bar{a}$ to another $\bar{a}^*$---into a sequence of conditional expectations that can each be estimated with BKMR.
Specifically, g-BKMR fits separate BKMR models for each time-varying confounder at each visit (mediator models) and for the final outcome (outcome model), all with component-wise variable selection.
These models are then combined via Monte Carlo g-computation: for each posterior draw, counterfactual confounder values are sequentially sampled forward in time under the intervention, and the outcome is predicted under the resulting full exposure--confounder history.
This yields a full posterior distribution over the ACE, with credible intervals that propagate uncertainty from all fitted models.
The approach inherits BKMR's strengths---nonparametric exposure-response surfaces, automatic detection of nonlinearity and interactions, and posterior inclusion probabilities for variable selection---while providing the formal causal interpretation that standalone BKMR lacks in the longitudinal setting.

Despite its methodological appeal, g-BKMR has remained difficult to use in practice.
The original implementation required researchers to manually construct time-indexed data matrices, fit and store each BKMR model separately, write custom g-computation loops, and post-process posterior samples---steps that are error-prone, time-consuming, and inaccessible to most applied investigators.
No production-quality, documented R package has been available to date.

We introduce \pkg{causalGBKMR}, an open-source R package that makes g-BKMR accessible to the environmental health research community.
The package is designed to be \emph{general-purpose}: users can specify an arbitrary number of time points, any number of mixture components and time-varying confounders, and custom intervention contrasts (e.g., user-defined exposure levels for $\bar{a}$ and $\bar{a}^*$, or quantile-based contrasts at any pair of percentiles).
Variable naming, baseline covariates, and MCMC tuning parameters are all user-configurable, allowing the same interface to serve studies ranging from two-visit designs to complex multi-visit cohorts.
Specifically, the package provides:
(i) automated data preparation and variable detection from user-supplied matrices, with support for user-provided covariate names and optional log-transformation;
(ii) a single-function interface (\fn{gbkmr\_run}) that handles the full g-computation pipeline---fitting sequential BKMR models, Monte Carlo sampling of counterfactual trajectories, and posterior summarisation---while exposing all key parameters (number of MCMC iterations, burn-in selection, Monte Carlo sample size, knot count) for user control;
(iii) a fast-BKMR engine\citep{sonabend_2024_scalable} that is automatically activated for large samples ($n > 2{,}000$), with user-configurable subset count and parallelisation, extending applicability to cohort sizes representative of MESA, ARIC, and similar studies; and
(iv) comprehensive documentation including a tutorial vignette, pkgdown website (\url{https://lyric98.github.io/causalGBKMR/}), and continuous-integration testing via GitHub Actions.
The package is freely available at \url{https://github.com/Lyric98/causalGBKMR}.

We evaluate \pkg{causalGBKMR} through three use cases: (1) validation by replication of the simulation study in \citet{chai_gbkmr}; (2) application to the Strong Heart Study (SHS), a prospective cohort of American Indian adults; and (3) a computational benchmark comparing standard and fast-BKMR engines at large sample sizes.
Section~\ref{sec:methods} briefly reviews the g-BKMR estimator and describes the package architecture; Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the three use cases; Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses findings, limitations, and future directions.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{The g-BKMR estimator}
\label{sec:gbkmr-estimator}

Consider $n$ subjects observed over $T$ visits ($t = 0, 1, \ldots, T-1$).
At each visit $t$, let $\mathbf{A}_t = (A_{t1}, \ldots, A_{tp})^\top$ denote a $p$-vector of mixture exposures and $\mathbf{L}_t = (L_{t1}, \ldots, L_{tq})^\top$ a $q$-vector of time-varying confounders.
Baseline covariates are collected in $\mathbf{C}_0$ and the outcome $Y$ is measured at the final visit.
We use overbars to denote histories: $\bar{\mathbf{A}}_t = (\mathbf{A}_0, \ldots, \mathbf{A}_t)$ and $\bar{\mathbf{L}}_t = (\mathbf{L}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{L}_t)$.

The causal estimand is the average causal effect (ACE) of an exposure-trajectory contrast, defined as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ace}
\text{ACE} = E\bigl[Y^{\bar{\mathbf{a}}^*}\bigr] - E\bigl[Y^{\bar{\mathbf{a}}}\bigr],
\end{equation}
where $Y^{\bar{\mathbf{a}}}$ is the potential outcome under a hypothetical intervention setting the full exposure history to $\bar{\mathbf{a}}$.
By default, \pkg{causalGBKMR} sets $\bar{\mathbf{a}}$ and $\bar{\mathbf{a}}^*$ to the 25th and 75th percentiles of all exposure variables, respectively, though users may specify arbitrary contrast values.

Under the standard g-formula identifiability assumptions---consistency, sequential exchangeability (no unmeasured confounding at each visit conditional on observed history), and positivity---the counterfactual mean can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gformula}
E\bigl[Y^{\bar{\mathbf{a}}}\bigr] = \int \cdots \int E\bigl[Y \mid \bar{\mathbf{A}} = \bar{\mathbf{a}},\, \bar{\mathbf{L}},\, \mathbf{C}_0\bigr] \prod_{t=1}^{T-1} f\bigl(\mathbf{L}_t \mid \bar{\mathbf{A}}_{t-1} = \bar{\mathbf{a}}_{t-1},\, \bar{\mathbf{L}}_{t-1},\, \mathbf{C}_0\bigr)\, d\bar{\mathbf{L}}\, dF(\mathbf{C}_0).
\end{equation}
Direct evaluation of Equation~\eqref{eq:gformula} is intractable because of the high-dimensional integration over $\bar{\mathbf{L}}$.
The g-BKMR approach\citep{chai_gbkmr} addresses this by modelling each conditional distribution with BKMR and evaluating the integral via Monte Carlo simulation.
Specifically, the algorithm proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Fit mediator models.} For each time-varying confounder $L_{tl}$ at each visit $t = 1, \ldots, T-1$, fit a BKMR model: $L_{tl} = h_{tl}(\bar{\mathbf{A}}_{t-1}, \bar{\mathbf{L}}_{t-1}) + \mathbf{C}_0^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}_{tl} + \epsilon_{tl}$.
  \item \textbf{Fit outcome model.} Fit a BKMR model for the outcome: $Y = h_Y(\bar{\mathbf{A}}, \bar{\mathbf{L}}) + \mathbf{C}_0^\top \boldsymbol{\theta} + \epsilon_Y$.
  \item \textbf{Monte Carlo g-computation.} For each posterior draw $j = 1, \ldots, J$ and Monte Carlo replicate $k = 1, \ldots, K$:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item Sequentially sample confounders forward in time: $L_{tl}^{(j,k)} \sim P(L_{tl} \mid \bar{\mathbf{A}}_{t-1} = \bar{\mathbf{a}}_{t-1},\, \bar{\mathbf{L}}_{t-1}^{(j,k)},\, \mathbf{C}_0)$ using posterior draw $j$ from the mediator models.
    \item Predict the outcome: $Y^{(j,k)} \sim P(Y \mid \bar{\mathbf{A}} = \bar{\mathbf{a}},\, \bar{\mathbf{L}}^{(j,k)},\, \mathbf{C}_0)$ using posterior draw $j$ from the outcome model.
  \end{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Estimate ACE.} Average over Monte Carlo replicates and posterior draws: $\widehat{\text{ACE}} = \frac{1}{J}\sum_{j=1}^{J}\bigl[\bar{Y}^{(j)}_{\bar{\mathbf{a}}^*} - \bar{Y}^{(j)}_{\bar{\mathbf{a}}}\bigr]$, where $\bar{Y}^{(j)}_{\bar{\mathbf{a}}} = K^{-1}\sum_k Y^{(j,k)}_{\bar{\mathbf{a}}}$.
\end{enumerate}
The posterior distribution over the $J$ differences provides a $95\%$ credible interval that naturally propagates uncertainty from all fitted models.

\subsection{Bayesian kernel machine regression with variable selection}
\label{sec:bkmr-varsel}

Each BKMR model in the g-BKMR pipeline takes the form $y_i = h(\mathbf{z}_i) + \mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta} + \epsilon_i$, where $h(\cdot)$ is an unknown smooth function of the kernel predictors $\mathbf{z}_i$ and $\mathbf{x}_i$ are covariates with linear effects\citep{bobb_bkmr_2015}.
The function $h$ is assigned a Gaussian process prior with a squared-exponential kernel:
$\text{Cov}\bigl(h(\mathbf{z}_i), h(\mathbf{z}_j)\bigr) = \tau^2 \exp\bigl\{-\sum_{m=1}^{M} \rho_m (z_{im} - z_{jm})^2\bigr\}$,
where the bandwidth parameters $\rho_m$ control the smoothness with respect to each component.
For computational efficiency when $n$ is moderate, \pkg{causalGBKMR} uses the predictive-process (knot) approximation\citep{bobb_bkmr_2018}, projecting the kernel matrix onto a set of $d$ representative knots ($d = 50$ by default) selected via the space-filling \code{cover.design} algorithm in \pkg{fields}, reducing per-iteration cost from $O(n^3)$ to $O(nd^2)$.

Component-wise variable selection is achieved through a hierarchical spike-and-slab prior on the bandwidth parameters: each $\rho_m$ is multiplied by a binary indicator $\delta_m \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\pi)$, so that $\delta_m = 0$ effectively removes component $m$ from the kernel\citep{bobb_bkmr_2015}.
The posterior mean of $\delta_m$---the posterior inclusion probability (PIP)---quantifies the evidence that component $m$ contributes to the exposure-response surface.
Inference proceeds via Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC), with Gibbs updates for $\boldsymbol{\beta}$, $\tau^2$, and $\sigma^2$, and Metropolis--Hastings steps for the kernel parameters.

\subsection{Fast BKMR via variational inference}
\label{sec:fast-bkmr}

Standard BKMR requires $O(n^3)$ kernel matrix operations per MCMC iteration, which becomes prohibitive for large cohorts.
The fast-BKMR engine \citep{sonabend_2024_scalable} addresses this through a divide-and-conquer strategy implemented in the \pkg{fbkmr} package: the dataset is randomly split into $M$ subsets of approximately equal size, standard BKMR is fit independently on each subset (optionally in parallel), and posterior summaries are combined via median aggregation.
The recommended per-subset sample size is approximately $1{,}000$ \citep{sonabend_2024_scalable}, with the constraint that $M \le \sqrt{n}$.

\pkg{causalGBKMR} selects the fitting engine automatically based on the analysis sample size: when $n \le 2{,}000$, standard BKMR with Gaussian-process knot approximation ($50$ knots by default) is used; when $n > 2{,}000$ and the \pkg{fbkmr} package is installed, the fast-BKMR engine is activated with $M = \max(5,\, \lfloor n / 1{,}000 \rfloor)$ subsets.
The threshold of $n = 2{,}000$ was chosen because (i) standard BKMR with knot approximation remains computationally tractable up to this scale (approximately $80$ minutes per model in our benchmark; Section~\ref{sec:usecase-fastbkmr}), (ii) fast-BKMR requires at least $M = 2$ subsets to be meaningful, and each subset should contain approximately $1{,}000$ observations per the author recommendation, yielding a minimum total sample of $2{,}000$, and (iii) the user retains the ability to override this default via the \code{engine} argument.
When $n > 2{,}000$ but \pkg{fbkmr} is not installed, the package falls back to standard BKMR and issues an informative message suggesting installation.

\subsection{Software architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\pkg{causalGBKMR} is organised into five modules (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}):

\begin{description}[nosep,leftmargin=1em]
  \item[Input validation] (\code{01-validation.R}) checks matrix dimensions, types, and missingness before analysis.
  \item[Data preparation] (\code{02-data-preparation.R}) converts user-supplied matrices $(\mathbf{Y}, \mathbf{Z}, \mathbf{X})$ into the wide-format data frame required by the g-BKMR pipeline, with optional log-transformation and user-specified covariate names.
  \item[Variable detection] (\code{03-variable-detection.R}) automatically identifies exposure and time-varying confounder columns from the prepared data, supporting multiple naming conventions (e.g., \code{bmi\_0}, \code{bmi\_1} or \code{td\_covariate1\_0}).
  \item[Core analysis] (\code{04-core-analysis.R}) implements the full g-BKMR algorithm: sequential BKMR model fitting (standard or fast engine), knot placement, Monte Carlo g-computation, and posterior aggregation.
  \item[User interface] (\code{05-user-interface.R}) provides \fn{gbkmr\_run}, a single-function entry point that orchestrates the full pipeline and returns a structured results object with \code{print} and \code{summary} methods.
\end{description}

The two main user-facing functions are:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \fn{prepare\_gbkmr\_data}: converts raw matrices into g-BKMR format. Required arguments are \code{Y} (outcome), \code{Z} (exposure matrix, $n \times pT$), \code{X} (covariate matrix, $n \times (qT + B)$, where $B$ is the number of baseline covariates), \code{time\_points}, \code{mixture\_components}, and \code{td\_covariates}. Users may additionally specify \code{td\_covariate\_names}, \code{baseline\_covariates}, and \code{log\_transform\_mixtures}.
  \item \fn{gbkmr\_run}: runs the g-BKMR analysis. Key tuning parameters include \code{iter} (MCMC iterations; default $15{,}000$), \code{K} (Monte Carlo samples; default $1{,}000$), \code{n\_knots} (default $50$), \code{engine} (\code{"auto"}, \code{"bkmr"}, or \code{"fastbkmr"}), and \code{a\_probs} (intervention quantiles; default $0.25$ vs.\ $0.75$). Users requiring full control may call \fn{run\_gbkmr\_panel} directly, which additionally exposes \code{sel} (post-burn-in indices), \code{n\_iter} (separate outcome-model iterations), custom intervention values \code{a\_vals}/\code{astar\_vals}, and per-model verbose output.
\end{itemize}

The returned object contains the ACE estimate with $95\%$ credible interval, counterfactual means, posterior samples, fitted BKMR model objects, and analysis metadata.
Listing~\ref{lst:example} shows a minimal end-to-end example.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Minimal example: data preparation and g-BKMR analysis.},label={lst:example}]
library(causalGBKMR)

# Prepare data (2 metals x 3 visits, 1 TD confounder, 1 baseline cov)
dat <- prepare_gbkmr_data(
  Y = outcome, Z = metals_matrix, X = covariates_matrix,
  time_points = 3, mixture_components = 2,
  td_covariates = 1, baseline_covariates = 1,
  td_covariate_names = "waist"
)

# Run g-BKMR (engine auto-selected based on sample size)
results <- gbkmr_run(
  data = dat, time_points = 3,
  iter = 15000, K = 1000, n_knots = 50
)

# View results
print(results)   # ACE, 95% CrI, counterfactual means
summary(results)  # + settings and significance
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Use case 2: Application to the Strong Heart Study}
\label{sec:usecase-shs}

% --- Lyric draft, derived from gbkmr_mi/application_section_draft.md

\paragraph{Study population and exposures.}
We applied \pkg{causalGBKMR} to data from the Strong Heart Study (SHS), a prospective cohort of American Indian adults with low to moderate exposure to metals and a high burden of cardiometabolic disease\citep{shs_lee_1990}. The analytic sample consisted of 679 participants with complete urinary metal measurements at three study visits (Visits 1--3) and fasting blood glucose at Visit 3. Six urinary metals were considered: selenium (Se), cadmium (Cd), molybdenum (Mo), tungsten (W), arsenic (As), and zinc (Zn); all were standardised to urinary creatinine and log-transformed prior to analysis. The mixture comprised 18 exposure variables (six metals $\times$ three visits).

The outcome was log-transformed fasting blood glucose at Visit~3 (right-skewed distributions, skewness $1.8$--$2.1$). Time-varying confounders were waist circumference and fasting glucose at Visits 1 and 2; baseline covariates included age, sex, body mass index, study centre, smoking status, and baseline kidney function (CKD-EPI eGFR). Missing values were handled by multiple imputation ($m = 5$, predictive mean matching). Within-metal cross-visit correlations ranged from $0.2$ to $0.6$ (Spearman; Figure~\ref{fig:corr}).

\paragraph{Joint mixture effect.}
The joint causal effect of shifting all 18 exposures from their 25th to a higher percentile was approximately monotonic in the upper half of the exposure distribution (Figure~\ref{fig:dr-joint}). The 95\% credible interval excluded zero above approximately the 45th percentile. At the 75th vs.\ 25th percentile contrast, the average causal effect was $1.01$ standardised units of log glucose (95\% CrI: $0.72$, $1.33$).

\paragraph{Single-variable effects.}
Per-metal IQR causal effects (Figure~\ref{fig:forest}) identified zinc as the dominant contributor: Zn(V1) $0.51$ SD (95\% CrI $0.28$, $0.75$), Zn(V2) $0.26$ SD ($0.03$, $0.51$), Zn(V3) $0.36$ SD ($0.20$, $0.55$). Selenium at Visit~1 had a positive effect of $0.19$ SD ($-0.01$, $0.36$; posterior probability of a positive effect $0.97$). Cadmium at Visit~1 was inversely associated ($-0.21$ SD; 95\% CrI $-0.37$, $-0.05$). Other metal-timepoints had credible intervals overlapping zero.

\paragraph{Sensitivity to baseline diabetes status.}
Excluding the 265 participants with baseline diabetes ($n = 414$) substantially attenuated the joint mixture effect (Figure~\ref{fig:dr-compare}). Two findings remained robust: Se(V1) ($0.07$ SD, 95\% CrI $0.004$, $0.15$) and Zn(V3) ($0.14$ SD, 95\% CrI $0.00$, $0.35$).

\subsection{Use case 3: Computational benchmark (BKMR vs.\ fast BKMR)}
\label{sec:usecase-fastbkmr}

\paragraph{Design.}
We evaluated the computational performance of the standard BKMR and fast-BKMR engines in \pkg{causalGBKMR} using the same longitudinal DGP as our simulation validation ($T = 3$ visits, $p = 4$ exposures per visit, one time-dependent confounder, binary outcome) at a sample size of $n = 6{,}000$, representative of large environmental cohorts such as MESA.
The g-BKMR pipeline requires fitting three separate BKMR models: mediator at $t = 1$ ($4$ $Z$ columns), mediator at $t = 2$ ($9$ $Z$ columns), and the outcome ($14$ $Z$ columns).
We compared four methods with fully aligned MCMC settings ($15{,}000$ iterations, variable selection enabled, $60\%$ burn-in):
(1) standard BKMR with $n = 500$ subsample (current default);
(2) standard BKMR with $n = 2{,}000$ subsample;
(3) standard BKMR with the full $n = 6{,}000$ sample; and
(4) fast BKMR with the full $n = 6{,}000$ sample ($10$ subsets of approximately $600$ observations each, $10$ parallel cores).
All BKMR models used $50$-knot Gaussian process approximation; fast BKMR used subset decomposition without knots.
Three replicates were run per method--model combination.

\paragraph{Computation time.}
Table~\ref{tab:timing} summarises per-model wall-clock times.
Standard BKMR at $n = 6{,}000$ required approximately $12$ hours per model ($37$ hours for the full g-formula pipeline), making it impractical for routine use.
Fast BKMR processed the same $6{,}000$ observations in approximately $49$ minutes total for all three models on $10$ cores---\textbf{$15\times$ faster} than standard BKMR at the same sample size, and faster even than standard BKMR at $n = 2{,}000$ ($\sim$79 minutes per model on a single core) while leveraging all available data.
At $n = 500$, standard BKMR with knots completed in approximately $6$ minutes per model regardless of the number of $Z$ columns, reflecting the insensitivity of the knot approximation to predictor dimensionality.
The speedup of fast BKMR derives from two factors: (i) subset decomposition ($n = 6{,}000$ split into $10$ subsets of $600$, each with $O(600^3)$ kernel cost versus $O(6{,}000^3)$) and (ii) parallelisation across $10$ cores, together yielding near-linear scaling.

\paragraph{Posterior accuracy.}
Beta coefficients for the baseline covariates (sex, waist circumference at baseline) were nearly identical between fast BKMR and standard BKMR at the same sample size (Table~\ref{tab:betas}).
For example, in the mediator $t = 1$ model, fast BKMR yielded $(\hat\beta_\text{sex},\, \hat\beta_\text{waist0}) = (-0.000,\, 0.515)$ versus $(-0.005,\, 0.517)$ for standard BKMR, both at $n = 6{,}000$.
Full-data methods (both BKMR and fast BKMR at $n = 6{,}000$) exhibited $5$--$10\times$ lower between-replicate variance compared to the subsampled BKMR at $n = 500$, reflecting the statistical efficiency gain from using the complete sample.

\paragraph{Variable selection.}
With variable selection enabled (\code{varsel = TRUE}) for all methods, posterior inclusion probabilities (PIPs) were examined for the mediator $t = 1$ model, where the true exposure-response depends only on exposures $M_1$ and $M_2$ (Table~\ref{tab:pips}).
Standard BKMR at $n = 6{,}000$ achieved perfect separation (PIPs of $1.00$ for $M_1$ and $M_2$; $\le 0.10$ for noise variables).
Fast BKMR correctly identified $M_1$ (PIP $= 1.00$) and ranked $M_2$ second (PIP $= 0.75$), with noise variables appropriately assigned low PIPs ($\le 0.16$).
The slightly lower PIP for $M_2$ under fast BKMR is an expected consequence of subset splitting and WASP aggregation, in which variable selection signals may be diluted across independently fitted subsets.

\paragraph{Software integration.}
In \pkg{causalGBKMR}, users can switch between standard and fast BKMR by setting \code{engine = "fastbkmr"} in \fn{gbkmr\_run}, or by leaving the default \code{engine = "auto"}, which activates fast BKMR automatically when $n > 2{,}000$ and the \pkg{fbkmr} package is installed.
The package handles fast-BKMR fit objects internally: during g-computation, \code{bkmr::SamplePred()} is called on a randomly selected subset at each MCMC iteration, as the function is compatible with individual subset fits (class \code{bkmrfit}) but not with the full \code{skmbayes} list object.
All downstream summary and visualisation code operates identically regardless of the engine used, so that users can scale from small pilot analyses to large cohort studies without modifying their analysis scripts.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

We introduced \pkg{causalGBKMR}, the first open-source R package providing a complete, validated, and scalable implementation of the g-BKMR estimator for causal inference with time-varying environmental mixtures.
The package was evaluated through three use cases: (i) successful replication of the simulation study in \citet{chai_gbkmr}, confirming implementation fidelity; (ii) application to the Strong Heart Study, identifying zinc and selenium as the primary metal contributors to fasting glucose; and (iii) a computational benchmark demonstrating that the fast-BKMR engine achieves a $15\times$ speedup over standard BKMR at $n = 6{,}000$ while producing nearly identical posterior estimates.

A key design goal of \pkg{causalGBKMR} is generality and ease of use.
The package accommodates arbitrary numbers of time points, mixture components, and time-varying confounders, and supports both quantile-based and user-defined intervention contrasts.
Automatic engine selection based on sample size, comprehensive input validation, and a single-function interface (\fn{gbkmr\_run}) reduce the burden on applied researchers who may not be familiar with the underlying MCMC machinery.
The package is accompanied by a tutorial vignette, a pkgdown documentation website (\url{https://lyric98.github.io/causalGBKMR/}), unit tests, and continuous-integration checks, following current best practices for scientific software development.

Linda+Arce will provide language and references; coordinate with the parallel application paper.}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations should be noted.
First, the current implementation focuses on continuous outcomes; binary outcome support is available through the underlying \pkg{bkmr} package but has not yet been fully integrated into the g-computation pipeline.
Second, like all g-formula approaches, the validity of the causal estimates depends on the sequential exchangeability assumption (no unmeasured time-varying confounding), which is untestable from observed data.
Third, while the fast-BKMR engine substantially reduces computation time, the subset-splitting approach produces slightly less sharp variable selection (PIPs) compared to standard BKMR on the same data, as selection signals may be diluted across independently fitted subsets.
Fourth, for mixtures with a very large number of components ($p \gg 10$), the BKMR kernel computation remains the bottleneck even with knot approximation; dimension reduction strategies may be needed in such settings.

\paragraph{Future directions.}
Planned extensions include full binary-outcome support in the g-computation pipeline, integration of \pkg{causalGBKMR} into a broader ``causal mixtures'' umbrella package (alongside BKMR-CMA and related methods), and exposure-response visualisation utilities.
We also anticipate that future versions of the \pkg{fbkmr} package may support the knot approximation within the subset-splitting framework, which would further reduce fast-BKMR computation time for high-dimensional mixtures.
